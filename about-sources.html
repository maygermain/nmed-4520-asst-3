About

The Messier catalogue was primarily compiled by Charles Messier and Pierre Méchain based on their discoveries in the mid to late 1700s. Some of these discoveries were added later in the 1900s, producing the catalogue as we know it today.

Messier was a comet hunter by profession, but he found objects that could be mistaken as comets, and was inspired to create a catalogue of these objects to minimize errors. He often broadly referred to these objects as "nebulae", faint fuzzies whose true nature wasn't discernable with the technology available at the time. Today, we know the catalogue to consist of nebulae, star clusters, galaxies, and even one double star.

The telescope used by Messier was simple, and all the galaxies in the catalogue can be seen with a small telescope in some capacity. Despite this, the galaxies range from 3.4-60,000 kilo light years away from us - a testament to the size of the universe we live in.
